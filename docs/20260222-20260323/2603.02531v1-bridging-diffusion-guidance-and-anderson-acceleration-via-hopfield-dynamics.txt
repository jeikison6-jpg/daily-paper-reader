# 1. Introduction 

The emergence of text-to-image (T2I) diffusion mod-els (Rombach et al., 2022b; Podell et al., 2023; Esser et al., 2024) has revolutionized the landscape of generative arti-ficial intelligence, achieving remarkable success in tasks ranging from static image synthesis (Xie et al., 2024; Labs, 2024) to dynamic video generation (Blattmann et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2024; Yang et al., 2024; Team, 2025). Despite their ubiquitous adoption, standard sampling processes in diffusion (Ho et al., 2020) and flow-matching models (Lip-man et al., 2022) often remain suboptimal. To address this, the field has entered a ”great guidance sampling era,” where various techniques are employed to refine the sampling tra-jectory. The cornerstone of this era is Classifier-Free Guid-ance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022), which enhances gen-eration quality by extrapolating between conditional and unconditional score estimates. By pushing the sampling di-rection toward the conditional distribution, CFG effectively improve generation quality, albeit at a significant cost. Despite its success, CFG is limited by dual-pass inference costs and the requirement for null-condition training, hinder-ing its use in few-step distilled or single-step models (Lin et al., 2024; Sauer et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2024). While recent training-free methods (Ahn et al., 2025; Hong, 2024; Rajabi et al., 2025; Kim, 2025) bypass training by perturb-ing internal representations, such as corrupting self-attention or token embeddings, they still require two forward passes. Consequently, these approaches are still difficult to integrate with few-step generation frameworks. To overcome this issue, attention-space extrapolation has emerged as a promising paradigm. Specifically, (Kim & Sim, 2025) viewing attention through the lens of sparse Hopfield networks, leverage noise-robustness to extrapo-late between sparse and dense maps. Similarly, (Chen et al., 2025) extrapolates attention signals from positive and nega-tive prompts. Despite these empirical gains in quality and efficiency, the underlying mechanics of attention-space ex-trapolation remain poorly understood. This lack of a formal theoretical framework leaves the field largely reliant on em-pirical heuristics. In this paper, we establish the missing link between empiri-cal heuristics and classical convergence theory for attention-space guidance in diffusion models. By framing attention dynamics in Modern Hopfield Networks (MHN) (Ram-sauer et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023a) as a fixed-point iteration, we prove that attention-space extrap-olation is a special realization of Anderson Acceleration (AA) (Walker & Ni, 2011). This discovery provides the first principled explanation for the success of attention-space guidance, transforming empirical observations into a theo-retically grounded framework for diffusion sampling. Building on this foundation, we introduce a Geometry-

Aware Attention Guidance ( GAG ). Inspired by geometric guidance principles (Sadat et al., 2024), we decompose at-tention updates into parallel and orthogonal components relative to the guidance direction. This decomposition al-lows us to stabilize the acceleration process by selectively amplifying beneficial updates while suppressing harmful fluctuations. We further provide a theoretical guarantee for this stability by analyzing the weak contraction property (Alber & Guerre-Delabriere, 1997) of attention dynamics. Extensive experiments across diverse diffusion backbones and guidance frameworks demonstrate that our plug-and-play approach consistently enhances generation quality and text alignment. Notably, our method exhibits superior perfor-mance in distilled and few-steps models, where traditional CFG is often inapplicable or suboptimal. Our main contri-butions are summarized as follows: • We bridge attention dynamics and Anderson Acceler-ation, framing attention extrapolation as a fixed-point acceleration process within MHN. • We introduce a novel attention extrapolation rule, called GAG, based on geometric decomposition that optimizes guidance trajectories by isolating beneficial updates, while rigorously establishing its stability via the weak contraction property of attention dynamics. • We demonstrate that our method achieves state-of-the-art performance across multiple diffusion backbones, showing high compatibility and effectiveness even in few-step generation regimes.

2.4. Acceleration of Fixed-Point Iteration 

For an operator F defined on a real Hilbert space H, a point x⋆ ∈ H is defined as a fixed point if x⋆ = F(x⋆).According to the Banach Fixed-Point Theorem (Banach, 1922), if F is a contraction mapping, the Picard iteration starting from any x0 ∈ H is guaranteed to converge to a unique fixed point: 

xk+1 = F(xk), k = 0 , 1, . . . (14) However, if F is merely nonexpansive, the Picard iteration may fail to converge and instead result in limit cycles. To address this, various acceleration techniques have been de-veloped, such as the Krasnosel’skii–Mann (KM) iteration (Mann, 1953), which employs a convex relaxation, and the inertial KM iteration (Maing ´e, 2008), which incorporates first-order momentum. # 3. Main Contribution 

In this section, we first formalize the retrieval dynamics of MHNs as a fixed-point iteration process. By addressing the inherent limitations of the standard Picard iteration in exist-ing MHN frameworks, we establish a theoretical foundation for attention extrapolation by framing it as an accelerated fixed-point process via the special case of AA ( Section 3.1). Building on this, we introduce a geometry-aware extrapo-lation rule( Section 3.3) and provide rigorous proofs for its stability and convergence( Section 3.4). 

3.1. Retrieval Dynamics as Fixed-Point Iteration 

We first revisit the retrieval dynamics T in MHNs (Ram-sauer et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2024), which are fundamentally Picard iterations, xk+1 = T (xk), designed to find a station-ary point x⋆ of the energy function that represents a stored memory. This relationship is formalized in the following: 

Theorem 3.1. (Ramsauer et al., 2020; Hu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023a) (Generalized Sparse Hopfield Retrieval Dynamics). The retrieval dynamics of the generalized sparse Hopfield model is a monotonic one-step update: 

xk+1 = T (xk) := Ξα-Entmax (βΞ⊤xk). (17) This implies that attention dynamics are the mechanism for finding fixed points, x⋆, in MHNs, where the standard attention layer performs exactly one iteration of this update rule. Within this framework, while previous works focus on im-proving the operator T via sparsity ( (Hu et al., 2024; Wu et al., 2023a), they remain restricted to the standard Picard iteration. We transcend this by framing the retrieval process through the lens of AA with m = 1 in Equation (16): 

xk+1 = T (xk) + ω (T (xk) − T (xk−1)) , (18) where ω is the extrapolation coefficient. In practical one-step update scenarios of MHN, the true previous iterate T (xk−1)

To ensure that acceleration does not deviate from the seman-tic target, we prove that extrapolation preserves the original fixed-point structure.

Remark 3.5 (Divergence in Non-Common Fixed Points) .

It is crucial to note that the preservation of the fixed point holds only under Assumption 3.3. In methods like NAG, the operators are conditioned on contrasting prompts (e.g., positive vs. negative), naturally leading to distinct attractors. In such cases, the discrepancy between two operators does not vanish at any single x∗. While this non-vanishing term acts as a ”guidance” force, assigning a weight matrix or scalar M (x) without a shared attractor can lead to unin-tended trajectory shifts or instability, as the extrapolation never reaches a stationary state.

# 6. Conclusion 

In this work, we uncover a missing link in diffusion guid-ance by establishing a rigorous theoretical foundation that connects attention-space extrapolation to MHN and AA. Building on this unification, we introduced a geometric de-composition method that stabilizes the acceleration process by decoupling updates into parallel and orthogonal compo-nents. Our plug-and-play approach significantly enhances generative quality across diverse models, including SDXL and Flux, without additional computational overhead. By bridging dynamical systems and diffusion guidance, this work not only provides a grounded framework for high-quality synthesis but also opens new horizons for viewing guidance as a fixed-point iteration problem.

To evaluate the robustness of our method across various guidance scales, we conduct conditional generation using Classifier-Free Guidance (CFG) (Ho & Salimans, 2022) with a default scale of 5.0. Regarding our rescaling parameter, we empirically set η = 15 across all experiments, which consistently yielded the best balance between visual quality and semantic alignment in our preliminary studies.

# E. Additional Qualitative Results 

In this section, we provide extended qualitative evaluations to demonstrate the effectiveness and versatility of our proposed method, GAG, across diverse generation tasks and in combination with various state-of-the-art guidance frameworks. 

Synergy with Existing Guidance Methods. Figures 4, 5, and 6 illustrate the qualitative impact of integrating GAG with established guidance techniques, including CFG (Ho & Salimans, 2022), APG (Sadat et al., 2024), and PAG (Ahn et al., 2025). Compared to vanilla guidance sampling and the comparable PLADIS baseline, our GAG-enhanced results exhibit a significant improvement in image plausibility. This is particularly evident in enhanced text-to-image alignment, improved coherence in complex prompts, and superior performance in challenging tasks such as object counting and visual effect rendering. These results confirm that GAG acts as a robust plug-and-play enhancement, elevating generation quality without requiring any additional inference steps. 

Application to Guidance-Distilled Models. Figures 7 and 8 present qualitative results from applying GAG to step-distilled models, such as DMD2 (Yin et al., 2024) and Hyper-SDXL (Ren et al., 2024), specifically in 4-step sampling regimes. We observe that GAG significantly mitigates unnatural artifacts and enhances semantic coherence between the text prompt and the generated image. Notably, GAG achieves these refinements while remaining nearly cost-free in terms of computational overhead, making it ideal for high-speed distilled architectures where inference efficiency is paramount. 

Robustness across Architectures: Extension to Flux. In Figures 9 and 10, we demonstrate GAG’s applicability to large-scale backbone models, including Flux.1-schnell (4 steps) and Flux.1-dev (28 steps). Our method proves effective not only for standard SDXL backbones but also for complex MMDiT architectures, showcasing exceptional robustness and architectural compatibility. GAG successfully refines visual details and prompt adherence even in these massive models, underscoring its generalizability and effectiveness across different model scales and training objectives. 

Comparative Analysis with NAG. Figure 11 provides a direct comparison between NAG (Chen et al., 2025), PLADIS, and GAG on the Hyper-SDXL framework for 4-step cases. While NAG offers enhanced guidance, it incurs substantial inference costs due to its additional computational requirements. In contrast, GAG delivers more visually pleasing results and superior text alignment without any additional inference overhead.
