# Abstract 

We present ReMoT , a unified training paradigm to sys-tematically address the fundamental shortcomings of VLMs in spatio-temporal consistency—a critical failure point in navigation, robotics, and autonomous driving. ReMoT 

integrates two core components: (i) A rule-based auto-matic framework that generates ReMoT -16K, a large-scale (16.5K triplets) motion-contrast dataset derived from video meta-annotations, surpassing costly manual or model-based generation. (ii) Group Relative Policy Optimization, which we empirically validate, yields optimal performance and data efficiency for learning this contrastive reasoning, far exceeding standard Supervised Fine-Tuning. We also construct the first benchmark for fine-grained motion con-trast triplets to measure a VLM’s discrimination of subtle motion attributes (e.g., opposing directions). The resulting model achieves SOTA performance on our new benchmark and multiple standard VLM benchmarks, culminating in a remarkable 25.1% performance leap on spatio-temporal reasoning tasks. 

# 1. Introduction 

Vision–Language Models (VLMs) have rapidly evolved into general-purpose perception systems that unify visual understanding and language reasoning [42, 47, 77, 92]. They are increasingly being deployed in critical domains involving physical world interaction, such as AIGC [22, 64, 81–84, 94, 100], embodied intelligence [13, 15, 16, 37, 50], and autonomous driving [10, 102]. These tasks fundamen-tally require models to move beyond static, single-frame perception and instead reason about how physical scenes evolve in space and time, for example, tracking 3D object motion [17, 118], interpreting dynamic changes across con-secutive frames [102], or inferring global orientation [45,

However, current mainstream VLMs [2, 9, 26, 46, 62] exhibit significant limitations in this regard: While they ex-cel at aligning visual semantics, they suffer from fundamen-tal deficiencies in the core capability of ensuring spatial-temporal consistency. Our empirical analysis, spanning diverse scenarios such as navigation, robotic manipulation, indoor explo-ration, and game simulation, reveals the pervasiveness of this shortcoming. However, existing methods, including architectural modifi-cations [36, 101] or data augmentation [14, 23, 65, 66], are largely limited to piecemeal fixes. They fail to provide a systematic solution that addresses this fundamental limita-tion across the paradigms of data, training, and evaluation. # 2. Related Work 

VLM Spatio-Temporal Reasoning Vision-language mod-els [9, 28, 46] have advanced significantly, yet bench-marks [60, 105, 106, 111] consistently reveal failures in spatio-temporal reasoning: VLMs perform adequately on egocentric spatial judgments but fail at allocentric view-point reasoning and cross-viewpoint comprehension [23, 55]. Works [17, 80] generate static 3D relationships through depth lifting and scene graphs, scaling to billions of QA pairs. Temporal reasoning methods employ time-aware en-coders [51], disentangled attention [5], or textual temporal transfer [56], yet struggle with temporal concepts and rely on temporal proximity rather than contrastive motion se-mantics. 3D/4D approaches integrate geometry priors via reconstructive tuning [36] or world model latents [86, 103], but static encoders weaken spatial grounding with sequen-tial cues [112] and require costly depth sensors. However, these methods tackle isolated aspects (data scale, architec-tural design, or evaluation metrics) without a unified frame-work addressing motion understanding holistically. Our work systematically addresses this gap through: (i) motion-contrast triplet construction from metadata, (ii) hybrid SFT-GRPO training for reasoning consistency, and (iii) allocen-tric evaluation protocols capturing fine-grained motion se-mantics. 

Data Construction for Motion Supervision Existing training data for motion understanding suffers from insuf-ficient granularity: video-text datasets provide only coarse clip-level captions [11, 40], while embodied datasets of-fer structured metadata (poses, actions), but limited seman-tic annotations [31, 76]. Recent advances explore self-supervised learning [3, 66], contrastive training [23, 33], and temporal modeling [78], yet they either focus on spatial geometry over motion semantics [66], require massive unla-beled video [3], or use temporal proximity as a weak motion proxy [33]—failing to explicitly model contrastive motion attributes. In contrast, our motion-contrast triplets explicitly encode directional semantics by pairing each anchor with a motion-aligned positive (e.g., “left rotation by 20°”) and a motion-opposing hard negative synthesized from meta-data. This enables scalable supervision for fine-grained motion discrimination unavailable in proximity-based or reconstruction-based paradigms. 

Training Paradigms for Reasoning Enhancement 

Vision-Language Models (VLMs) for spatio-temporal reasoning typically employ supervised fine-tuning (SFT) with techniques like LoRA [44], yet struggle with long-horizon consistency due to limited reasoning data [41]. Reinforcement learning paradigms address these limita-tions through Direct Preference Optimization (DPO) [75] and Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) [43], which demonstrate significant improvements in chain-of-thought generation for visual domains [24, 73, 116]. Hybrid strategies combine SFT with RL through preference learning [87], yet systematic explorations across diverse data sources remain limited [58, 99]. We further explore hybrid optimization schedules beyond standard sequential integration (SFT →GRPO) [87], introducing an alternating strategy (SFT ↔GRPO) that jointly evolves linguistic fluency and reward alignment. 

# 3. Method 

## 3.1. ReMoT-16k Data Construction 

Existing video datasets predominantly provide clip-level captions or static scene descriptions, lacking explicit super-vision for distinguishing physically valid frame transitions from invalid ones. To address this gap, we propose ReMoT -16k that systematically generates motion-contrast triplets 

from videos with meta-annotations. 

Motion-Contrast Triplets. Each triplet consists of 

(Ianchor , I pos , I neg ), where the anchor-positive pair exhibits a specific motion property m while the anchor-negative pair violates this property despite visual similarity. This design forces models to learn fine-grained motion discrimination rather than relying on superficial visual patterns. Construc-tion requires three critical capabilities: (1) extracting mo-tion properties from frame sequences, (2) constructing mo-tion contrast triplets, and (3) generating VQA formulations. The most straightforward approach is leveraging vision-language models as unified agents to perform all three stages. We explore this direction using state-of-the-art LVLMs (Qwen-3-VL, Gemini-2.5-Pro), prompting them to analyze video frames and metadata, identify salient motion patterns, and generate structured triplets in JSON format. However, practical deployment reveals severe limita-tions. Manual inspection shows 55% of outputs contain format violations (e.g., mismatched option counts). The ap-proach also incurs prohibitive API costs and time overhead. After quality filtering, we obtain 632 valid triplets. These challenges motivate a more systematic alternative.

We then integrate the 100 VLM-generated triplets covering object tracking and game scenarios, forming a final benchmark of 600 eval-uation triplets, with 1776 total evaluation questions. The benchmark exhibits high visual hardness.

## 4.3. Ablation and Comparative Studies 

Training Data Composition. Table 2 shows incremen-tal improvements from different task types. Starting from the Qwen3-VL baseline (20.7%), manipulation data pro-vides modest gains (+3.25%), while adding navigation data brings substantial improvement to 32.35% (+8.4%), indi-cating spatial relation reasoning is crucial. Incorporating simulation data further achieves 38.0% (+5.65%). 

Logic Reward Decoupling. Table 3 validates our ex-plicit logic supervision on the Manipulation subset. The logic-ability test data are mainly drawn from the Manip-ulation subset of our benchmark, which specifically mea-sures reasoning consistency. Therefore, the base model performance here may differ slightly from the overall re-sults reported in Table 1. We compare three settings: The base model shows poor logic correctness (46.62%), GRPO without logic reward improves this to 98.6% but achieves limited accuracy (68.6%), while our decoupled logic re-ward reaches 99.3% logic correctness and 78.0% accuracy (+10.6%). This strong correlation demonstrates that main-taining self-consistent reasoning chains is crucial for per-formance improvement. 

Data Scaling Analysis. Figure 3 compares scaling be-haviors between pipelines. Our multi-expert construction (Figure 3a) exhibits smooth scaling from 0.42 to 0.66 with no saturation, while VLM-generated data (Figure 3b) shows volatile fluctuations and plateaus at ∼0.49. This demonstrates that construction quality fundamentally limits achievable performance regardless of scale. Both pipelines show declining response verbosity as data increases.

## 8.1. Ablation Study 

8.1.1. Triplet vs. Binary Contrast 

To validate the necessity of triplet-based motion contrast, we conduct an ablation study comparing our triplet formulation against a binary (pairwise) alternative. This ablation directly examines whether our (Ianchor , I pos , I neg ) triplet formulation is superior to constructing separate binary pairs (Ianchor , I pos ) and (Ianchor , I neg ) for learning fine-grained spatio-temporal discrimination. 

Experimental Setup. In the binary variant, we decompose each triplet into two separate binary pairs: a positive pair 

(Ianchor , I pos ) retaining motion property m, and a negative pair (Ianchor , I neg ) with conflicting property ¯m. The VQA formula-tion is simplified to independent binary verification questions for each pair (e.g., “Does the camera rotate left between these frames?”), treating positive and negative examples as isolated training instances rather than contrastive options within the same question. Both variants use identical training data scale, the same GRPO training procedure, and equivalent computa-tional budgets. 

Results. Table 6 presents the performance comparison on our ReMoT-16k-Test benchmark. The triplet-based approach substantially outperforms the binary baseline, achieving 38.0% Overall accuracy compared to 19.4% for binary pairs—a remarkable +18.6% absolute improvement. On the Partial accuracy metric, triplet learning yields 64.0% versus 39.4%, representing a +24.6% gain.            

> Table 6. Ablation study comparing triplet-based and binary-based contrast learning on ReMoT-16k-Test benchmark. The triplet formulation with joint contrastive supervision demonstrates substantial advantages across both evaluation metrics.
> Contrast Formulation Overall (%) Partial (%)
> Binary Pairs (Ianchor , I pos ),(Ianchor , I neg )19.4 39.4 Triplets (Ianchor , I pos , I neg )38.0 64.0
> Absolute Gain +18.6 +24.6

Analysis. The dramatic performance gap underscores the critical importance of joint contrastive supervision in learning fine-grained motion discrimination. While the binary baseline exposes the model to both positive and negative examples through separate (Ianchor , I pos ) and (Ianchor , I neg ) pairs, treating them as isolated instances fails to establish explicit contrastive relationships, making it difficult for the model to learn the relative motion relationships within the scene. In contrast, our triplet formulation jointly presents both transitions—one exhibiting motion property m (Ipos ) and another exhibiting the reversed or conflicting property ¯m (Ineg )—for the same anchor frame. Critically, both Ipos and Ineg depict physically plausible transitions; the distinction lies in their opposing motion directions or attributes relative to the anchor (e.g., leftward vs. rightward camera rotation, approach vs. retract robot trajectories). This design enables the model to directly learn contrastive boundaries through comparative reasoning—simultaneously evaluating opposing motion patterns—forcing explicit discrimination of fine-grained directional differences even when both alternatives are visually similar due to identical scene content. 

8.1.2. GRPO Rollout Configuration 

Group Relative Policy Optimization (GRPO) typically employs a rollout size of 8 for generating multiple response candidates per query. However, to improve training efficiency and reduce memory consumption, we train our model with a rollout size of 4. To validate this design choice, we conduct an ablation study comparing rollout sizes of 4 and 8 under identical training conditions. 

Experimental Setup. For rapid validation, we train both variants on 1/10 of the full training dataset using the same hyperparameters, learning rate, and training steps. The only difference is the number of response candidates sampled per query during GRPO training: 4 for our configuration vs. 8 for the standard setting. Both models are evaluated on the ReMoT-16k-Test benchmark. This reduced-scale experiment allows us to efficiently compare rollout configurations without the computational cost of full-scale training. 

Results. Table 7 presents the performance comparison. The performance difference is negligible (0.62% on Overall, +0.65% on Partial for rollout 4), demonstrating that reducing rollout size does not significantly impact model quality in our setting.

8.1.3. Optimization Strategy: GRPO vs. DPO and DAPO Experimental Setup. We compare three representative optimization strategies—DPO, DAPO, and GRPO—using Qwen3-VL-4B as the base model under identical training data and computational budgets. We additionally compare two reward formulations within GRPO: learned rewards (LR) and rule-based rewards (RR). 

Results. Table 8 summarizes the results. DPO achieves only 23.8% overall accuracy, while DAPO reaches 51.6% and our GRPO (RR) achieves 61.1% .

Analysis. DPO optimizes over static preference pairs, which is ineffective for tasks requiring long and precise chains of reasoning and lacks the fine-grained supervision needed to identify specific errors. In contrast, GRPO samples multiple trajectories and computes group-relative advantages, enabling the model to explore varied spatial interpretations and learn from relative quality among its own outputs rather than manually curated negatives. This flexibility is critical for spatial reasoning, where errors manifest diversely (e.g., depth inconsistencies, left-right confusion). Regarding reward formulation, we use rule-based rewards (RR) because spatial facts—such as 3D coordinates and motion directions—are deterministic and geometrically verifiable, yielding noise-free oracle supervision. GRPO-LR achieves only 32.3% overall accuracy, substantially below GRPO-RR (61.1%) across all task categories. Furthermore, per-step reward inference introduces significant overhead: 26h/epoch for LR vs. 3.5h for RR (7.4 × slower) . This confirms that determin-istic geometric verification provides more effective and efficient supervision signals than preference-based learned reward modeling.

## 8.2. Analysis of Reasoning Chain Faithfulness 

To assess the quality of generated reasoning chains, we randomly sample 25 test outputs and manually annotate whether the reasoning chain is logically consistent with the final answer. The base model Qwen3-VL-4B-CoT exhibits reasoning errors in approximately 60% of cases, whereas ReMoT reduces this to 12% .A representative failure case illustrates the issue: the CoT states “ ...left arm moving up and right → A... ” (incorrect direction) while producing <answer>B</answer> (correct answer), where the reasoning chain directly contradicts the final prediction. This reflects a known decoupling problem: reasoning traces can serve as post-hoc rationalizations rather than genuinely guiding predictions when models are trained with final-answer supervision alone. ReMoT mitigates this decoupling (60% → 12% ) via composite rewards under GRPO that jointly optimize reasoning coherence and answer correctness across the entire response, rather than supervising only the final token.

# 9. Limitations and Future Work 

While ReMoT demonstrates significant improvements in spatio-temporal reasoning, we acknowledge two primary limitations that outline directions for future research. 

Base Model Selection and Generalizability. Our experiments primarily utilize the Qwen3-VL-4B-Thinking architecture. This selection was deliberate rather than arbitrary; Qwen3-VL is currently recognized as possessing state-of-the-art spatial understanding capabilities among open-source models. Since our proposed GRPO-based optimization operates by refining the model’s internal reasoning chains (Chain-of-Thought) rather than injecting new knowledge from scratch, a strong intrinsic spatial prior is a prerequisite for stable RL convergence and effective self-correction. Consequently, we prioritized validating the ReMoT paradigm on the most capable available base model to establish a high-performance ceiling. 

Scalability to In-the-Wild Videos. The construction of ReMoT-16K currently relies on datasets with basic meta-annotations (e.g., SE(3) camera poses in ScanNet, robot telemetry in AgiBot). While this ensures the logical rigorousness of our hard negative samples, it constrains the immediate scalability of our pipeline to massive, unannotated in-the-wild videos. We envision future work to bridge this gap by integrating off-the-shelf Structure-from-Motion (SfM) or SLAM systems (e.g., DROID-SLAM) into our pipeline. By generating pseudo-ground-truth motion metadata for web videos, we can scale the motion-contrast triplet paradigm to millions of samples, potentially unlocking broader generalization capabilities.
